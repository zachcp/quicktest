FAQs

Molecules Biosynthesis Antibiotic Biosynthesis Nagoya Protocol

Q: Are you finding Molecules?

A: Sort of, but no. In this study we are looking at genes that are involved in the biosynthesis of small molecules. These genes can be used to guide the discovery of small molecules, however, in this study we are simply using this genes to look for variation across samples and look for ecological trends.

Q: How does biosynthesis work?

A: Thats a big question. The short answer is "enzymes". The longer answer is that living things have evolved exquisite mechanisms for transforming simple chemicals into complex molecules via catalysts. This transformation of molecules occurs in all living things all around you, all the time. Whats different in bacteria is that they have retained a much wider range of transformations that "higher" organism which often forego the need to encode these transformations in the genome by acquiring nutrients by eating things lower ont he food chain. perhaps most well known are human's inability to make the aromatic amino acids; we must acquire these from our diet. Of course there are lots of other micronutrients we cannot make ..

Q: But how does antibiotic biosynthesis work?

A: Another great question. Most of the interesting bioactive small molecules that have become potent members of our pharmacy - things like penicillin, rapamycin, vancomycin, epothilone - are made from simple precursors that we are familiar with, namely amino acids and fatty acids. However, unlike in our bodies where these precursors usually end up as molecules we are familiar with (proteins and fats), bacteria are able to create a much wider array of structures. The collection of enzymes required for the synthetic recipe are usually found localized together on the bacterial chromosome and the knowledge of the genes in this gene collective, or gene cluster, allows us to have a pretty good idea of the molecule that is encoded by these genes, even in the absence of the molecule. The actual synthesis of compounds occurs via enzymatic reactions, and the two largest families of bioactive metabolites, Non Ribosomal Peptides and Polyketides are actually synthesized while tethered to a large enzyme, that passes the molecules along the core enzyme.

Q: Does this study follow the Nagoya Protocol?

A: Absolutely. The Nagoya Protocol is an international agreement governing the use of the world's biodiversity. This is a purely observational study which we hope will guide future molecule discovery efforts that are beyond the scope of this project. Therefore, in keeping with the best practices of the Nagoya Protocol on biodiversity, we will not archive the genetic information contained in any soil nor will we culture any organisms from any soil samples we receive. We will deposit all data obtained in this study on a publicly accessible resource.
